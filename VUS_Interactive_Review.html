VUS.1

Historical Thinking Skills — Always on the Exam!

Primary Source
👆 Tap to flip
Created DURING the time period — diaries, speeches, letters, photos, laws, political cartoons. The author was present or alive at the time.
Secondary Source
👆 Tap to flip
Written AFTER the event using primary sources as evidence — textbooks, biographies, documentaries. The author was not present.
Point of View / Bias
👆 Tap to flip
The author's perspective — WHO wrote it and WHY affects the message. All sources have bias; identify it to evaluate evidence accurately.
Corroborate
👆 Tap to flip
Check if MULTIPLE sources agree. When several independent sources say the same thing, the evidence is stronger and more reliable.
Cause
👆 Tap to flip
WHY something happened — the conditions or decisions that led to a result. Exam often asks for the 'primary cause.'
Effect
👆 Tap to flip
WHAT happened as a result. Chains: event → effect → next event → next effect.
Continuity
👆 Tap to flip
Things that STAYED THE SAME over time. Example: the Constitution as the framework of government.
Change Over Time
👆 Tap to flip
Things that became DIFFERENT. Women's rights, role of government, and technology show major changes across VUS standards.
Historical Context
👆 Tap to flip
The broader background at the time the source was created. Context explains WHY something was significant.
HAPP Framework
👆 Tap to flip
Historical Context, Audience, Purpose, Point of View. Apply to EVERY primary source on the exam.

VUS.2

The Founding Era (1770s–1800s)

Declaration of Independence (1776)
👆 Tap to flip
Declared colonies free from Britain. Core idea: all men have natural rights to life, liberty, pursuit of happiness. Based on Enlightenment (John Locke).
Articles of Confederation
👆 Tap to flip
America's FIRST government — TOO WEAK. No power to tax, no executive, no courts. Each state = 1 vote. Shays' Rebellion (1786) revealed its failure.
Constitutional Convention (1787)
👆 Tap to flip
Meeting in Philadelphia where 55 delegates wrote the new Constitution. Originally called just to revise the Articles — instead created an entirely new government.
Federalism
👆 Tap to flip
Sharing power between NATIONAL and STATE governments. Federal law is supreme (Supremacy Clause), but states retain reserved powers (10th Amendment).
Separation of Powers
👆 Tap to flip
3 branches: Legislative (Congress makes laws), Executive (President enforces), Judicial (Courts interpret). Prevents any one branch from becoming tyrannical.
Checks & Balances
👆 Tap to flip
Each branch LIMITS the others. President vetoes → Congress overrides. Courts strike down laws. Senate confirms appointments. No branch is supreme.
Federalists
👆 Tap to flip
Supported the Constitution and strong national government. Leaders: Hamilton, Madison, Jay. Wrote The Federalist Papers to argue for ratification.
Anti-Federalists
👆 Tap to flip
OPPOSED the Constitution — feared strong central government = tyranny. Demanded a Bill of Rights first. Leaders: Patrick Henry, George Mason.
Bill of Rights
👆 Tap to flip
First 10 Amendments (1791). Added to satisfy Anti-Federalist demand. Protects speech, religion, press, assembly, due process, right to bear arms, and more.
Great Compromise
👆 Tap to flip
Resolved big vs. small state debate: Senate = 2 per state (equal). House = by population (proportional). Created the bicameral Congress.
3/5 Compromise
👆 Tap to flip
Enslaved people counted as 3/5 of a person for population counts — NOT citizenship. Gave Southern slave states more seats in Congress and Electoral College votes.
Popular Sovereignty
👆 Tap to flip
Government power comes FROM THE PEOPLE. 'We the People...' — citizens consent to be governed and can change their government through voting and amendments.

Constitutional Principles — The Exam Loves These
★
Federalism: Power shared between national and state governments. Federal law supreme, but states retain reserved powers.
★
Separation of Powers: Three equal branches — Legislative, Executive, Judicial. Each has specific, limited powers.
★
Checks & Balances: President vetoes → Congress overrides. Courts strike down laws. Senate confirms appointments.
★
Popular Sovereignty: All government power comes from the consent of the governed. Citizens can vote, petition, and amend the Constitution.
★
Limited Government: The government can only do what the Constitution authorizes. The Bill of Rights lists specific things government CANNOT do.
Key Compromises at the Convention
🔑
Great Compromise: Senate (equal — 2 per state) + House (proportional — by population). Solved the big vs. small state debate.
🔑
3/5 Compromise: Enslaved people = 3/5 of a person for representation. Gave Southern states MORE Congressional seats and Electoral College votes.
🔑
Slave Trade Compromise: Congress could not ban the international slave trade until 1808. Delayed abolition by decades.

The Early Republic (1790s–1820s)

Precedent
👆 Tap to flip
A decision or action that becomes a model for the future. Washington set many: 2-term limit, Cabinet system, neutral foreign policy, Farewell Address.
Federalist Party
👆 Tap to flip
Hamilton's party: strong national government, national bank, manufacturing, LOOSE interpretation of the Constitution, pro-British foreign policy.
Democratic-Republican Party
👆 Tap to flip
Jefferson's party: states' rights, farming economy, STRICT interpretation of the Constitution, pro-French foreign policy.
Strict vs. Loose Construction
👆 Tap to flip
STRICT (Jefferson): Government can ONLY do what the Constitution specifically says. LOOSE (Hamilton): Government can do what is 'necessary and proper' — implied powers.
Judicial Review
👆 Tap to flip
Power of the Supreme Court to declare laws UNCONSTITUTIONAL. NOT in the Constitution — Chief Justice John Marshall established it in Marbury v. Madison (1803).
Marbury v. Madison (1803)
👆 Tap to flip
Established judicial review. Made the Supreme Court a true co-equal branch. The most important Supreme Court precedent in history.
Louisiana Purchase (1803)
👆 Tap to flip
Jefferson bought land from France, DOUBLING the U.S. size for $15 million. Ironic — he believed in strict interpretation, but the Constitution doesn't mention buying land!
Monroe Doctrine (1823)
👆 Tap to flip
Europe must stay OUT of the Western Hemisphere. U.S. will not interfere with existing colonies. Basis of U.S. foreign policy in the Americas for over a century.
War of 1812
👆 Tap to flip
Second war with Britain. Causes: impressment of sailors, trade interference, British support for Native Americans. Results: American nationalism surged, Era of Good Feelings.
Washington's Key Precedents (1789–1797)
PrecedentWhy It Mattered
Served only TWO TERMSSet a tradition — formalized in the 22nd Amendment (1951). Prevented presidency from becoming an elected monarchy.

Hamilton vs. Jefferson — The Core Debate
🔑
This debate never ended: Hamilton wanted strong federal government; Jefferson wanted states' rights. These two visions still define American politics today.
🔑
National Bank controversy: Hamilton proposed it; Jefferson said it was unconstitutional. Hamilton used the 'necessary and proper' clause. Washington sided with Hamilton — bank was created.

Sectionalism, Slavery & Reform (1820s–1850s)

Sectionalism
👆 Tap to flip
Loyalty to your REGION (North, South, West) OVER the country. Slavery was the root cause — each region had different economic interests.
Missouri Compromise (1820)
👆 Tap to flip
Missouri = slave; Maine = free. Slavery BANNED north of 36°30'. Temporarily resolved sectional tensions — lasted 34 years until K-N Act.
Nullification Crisis (1832)
👆 Tap to flip
South Carolina declared it could VOID federal tariff laws. Jackson said nullification = TREASON. Resolved by compromise but planted states' rights argument the South would use again.
Manifest Destiny
👆 Tap to flip
Belief that the U.S. was DESTINED to expand across North America to the Pacific. Justified westward expansion, Mexican-American War, and displacement of Native peoples.
Compromise of 1850
👆 Tap to flip
CA = free state. New territories use popular sovereignty. Stronger Fugitive Slave Law (made Northerners return escaped enslaved people). Slave trade banned in D.C.
Kansas-Nebraska Act (1854)
👆 Tap to flip
Repealed Missouri Compromise. Popular sovereignty in Kansas and Nebraska → violent conflict ('Bleeding Kansas'). Accelerated path to Civil War.
Dred Scott v. Sandford (1857)
👆 Tap to flip
Supreme Court: enslaved people = PROPERTY, not citizens. Congress CANNOT ban slavery in territories. Outraged the North. Made Civil War virtually inevitable.
Abolitionism
👆 Tap to flip
Movement to END SLAVERY. Leaders: Garrison (The Liberator), Frederick Douglass (escaped slave, speaker), Harriet Tubman (Underground Railroad), Sojourner Truth.
Underground Railroad
👆 Tap to flip
Secret network helping enslaved people escape to freedom. Harriet Tubman made 19 trips and freed 300+ people. 'Conductors' risked imprisonment.
Seneca Falls Convention (1848)
👆 Tap to flip
First women's rights convention. Declaration of Sentiments demanded equal rights including voting. Led by Elizabeth Cady Stanton and Lucretia Mott.

The Civil War (1860–1865)

Secession
👆 Tap to flip
11 Southern states LEFT the Union after Lincoln's election to form the Confederate States of America (CSA). Jefferson Davis = President. Lincoln declared it illegal.
Confederate States of America
👆 Tap to flip
The breakaway Southern nation (1861–1865). Capital: Richmond, Virginia. Its Constitution explicitly protected slavery throughout all Confederate territory.
Fort Sumter (April 1861)
👆 Tap to flip
Confederate forces attacked this U.S. fort in South Carolina — the FIRST battle of the Civil War. The South fired first, helping Lincoln rally Northern support.
Emancipation Proclamation (1863)
👆 Tap to flip
Freed enslaved people in REBEL states only (not border states, not Union-controlled areas). Changed war's purpose. Allowed Black soldiers. Prevented European support for the Confederacy.
Total War
👆 Tap to flip
Military strategy targeting BOTH soldiers AND civilian infrastructure and morale. Sherman's March to the Sea (1864): burned Atlanta, marched to Savannah, destroyed a 60-mile-wide path.
Gettysburg Address (1863)
👆 Tap to flip
Lincoln's 272-word speech after the battle. Referenced 1776 and the Declaration of Independence. Redefined the war as a fight for equality and democracy.
13th Amendment (1865)
👆 Tap to flip
ABOLISHED SLAVERY throughout the entire United States. Made the Emancipation Proclamation permanent and nationwide. Remember: 13 = slavery abolished.
Appomattox Court House (April 1865)
👆 Tap to flip
Where Lee surrendered to Grant on April 9, 1865. Effectively ended the Civil War. Grant allowed Confederate soldiers to keep horses and go home peacefully.
ROOT CAUSE: The Civil War Was About Slavery
🔑
Don't be fooled by 'states' rights': The right the South was fighting for WAS the right to own enslaved people. Confederate VP Alexander Stephens called slavery the 'cornerstone' of the Confederacy.
North vs. South — Strengths and Weaknesses
Union (North) — ADVANTAGES
Confederacy (South) — ADVANTAGES
  • Larger population (22M vs. 9M)
  • Industrial economy — factories, railroads
  • Navy — blockaded southern ports
  • More miles of railroad
  • Financial resources and credit
  • Fighting on home territory (defensive war)
  • Excellent military leadership (Lee, Jackson)
  • Strong military culture and motivation
  • Just needed to survive — not conquer
  • Hoped Britain/France would recognize them
Significance of the Emancipation Proclamation
1
Changed the war's purpose: Made the war explicitly about ending slavery — not just preserving the Union.
2
Allowed Black soldiers: 180,000+ Black men served in the Union Army and Navy after the Proclamation.
3
Prevented European support for South: Britain and France — which had abolished slavery — could no longer politically support the Confederacy.
4
Limited scope: Only freed enslaved people in REBEL states — NOT in border states or Union-controlled areas. KEY distinction for the exam.

VUS.6

Reconstruction (1865–1877)

Reconstruction
👆 Tap to flip
The period (1865–1877) when the U.S. tried to reunite the country and integrate formerly enslaved people into society as free citizens. It ultimately failed.
13th Amendment (1865)
👆 Tap to flip
ABOLISHED SLAVERY throughout the United States. Remember: 13 = slavery abolished.
14th Amendment (1868)
👆 Tap to flip
CITIZENSHIP and EQUAL PROTECTION for all born in the U.S. Overturned Dred Scott. Its Equal Protection Clause has been used in civil rights cases for 150+ years. Remember: 14 = citizenship.
15th Amendment (1870)
👆 Tap to flip
RIGHT TO VOTE cannot be denied based on race, color, or previous condition of servitude. Gave Black men the vote. Remember: 15 = voting rights for Black men.
Freedmen's Bureau
👆 Tap to flip
Federal agency helping formerly enslaved people with food, education, employment, and medical care. Established schools and colleges. Undermined by lack of funding and Southern resistance.
Sharecropping
👆 Tap to flip
Black farmers rented land from white landowners and paid with a share of their crop — often 50%. Kept many in perpetual debt (debt peonage). A new form of economic control replacing slavery.
Black Codes
👆 Tap to flip
Laws passed immediately after the war by Southern states limiting Black people's rights — controlling where they could work, live, and travel. Congress responded with Radical Reconstruction.
Ku Klux Klan (KKK)
👆 Tap to flip
Terrorist organization using murder, threats, and intimidation to prevent Black Americans from voting, owning property, and exercising rights. Supported by many white Southerners.
Compromise of 1877
👆 Tap to flip
Hayes became president; federal troops withdrawn from the South. Ended Reconstruction. Southern Democrats immediately reclaimed power and began dismantling Black rights.
Jim Crow Laws
👆 Tap to flip
State and local laws enforcing racial segregation (1877–1965). Separate schools, restaurants, transportation, restrooms. Enforced by violence. Dismantled by Civil Rights Act (1964).
Plessy v. Ferguson (1896)
👆 Tap to flip
'Separate but equal' facilities are constitutional. Gave legal backing to Jim Crow for 58 years — until Brown v. Board of Education (1954) overturned it.

Industrialization, Gilded Age & Progressivism (1870s–1920s)

Gilded Age
👆 Tap to flip
Late 1800s period of rapid industrial growth, enormous wealth for a few, and massive poverty for many. 'Gilded' = gold on the outside but rotten underneath (Mark Twain).
Robber Barons
👆 Tap to flip
Wealthy industrialists using ruthless tactics. Carnegie (steel — vertical integration), Rockefeller (oil — horizontal integration), Morgan (banking), Vanderbilt (railroads).
Monopoly / Trust
👆 Tap to flip
MONOPOLY = one company controls an industry. TRUST = combination of companies under one board. Rockefeller's Standard Oil controlled 90% of U.S. oil.
Vertical Integration
👆 Tap to flip
Carnegie's strategy: controlling ALL stages of production — iron ore mines to steel mills to transportation. Cuts out middlemen, lowers costs, eliminates competitors.
Horizontal Integration
👆 Tap to flip
Rockefeller's strategy: buying out ALL COMPETITORS in the same industry. Standard Oil bought every other oil company until it controlled 90% of the market.
Social Darwinism
👆 Tap to flip
Applying Darwin's 'survival of the fittest' to society — wealthy people are naturally superior. Used to JUSTIFY inequality and oppose government help for workers.
Labor Union
👆 Tap to flip
Workers organizing together to demand better wages, hours, and conditions. AFL (Samuel Gompers), Knights of Labor. Methods: strikes, collective bargaining.
Populist Party
👆 Tap to flip
Party representing struggling farmers (1890s). Demanded: silver currency, railroad regulation, direct election of senators, graduated income tax. Absorbed by Democrats.
Muckraker
👆 Tap to flip
Journalists exposing problems. Sinclair (meatpacking → Pure Food Act), Tarbell (Standard Oil → antitrust enforcement), Riis (NYC poverty → housing reform).
Sherman Antitrust Act (1890)
👆 Tap to flip
First federal law to break up monopolies. Initially weakly enforced — Theodore Roosevelt used it aggressively as 'Trust Buster.' Broke up Standard Oil (1911).
Progressive Era
👆 Tap to flip
Reform movement (1890s–1920s) targeting monopolies, corruption, unsafe working conditions, and inequality. Used legislation, constitutional amendments, and investigative journalism.

Imperialism & World War I (1890s–1920s)

Imperialism
👆 Tap to flip
Powerful country extends control over weaker ones — politically, economically, or militarily. The U.S. debated whether imperialism was consistent with American values.
Spanish-American War (1898)
👆 Tap to flip
U.S. defeated Spain. Causes: Cuban independence movement, USS Maine explosion, yellow journalism. RESULTS: U.S. gained Philippines, Puerto Rico, Guam; Cuba = independent. U.S. became a world power.
Yellow Journalism
👆 Tap to flip
Sensational, exaggerated news by Hearst and Pulitzer to sell papers and stir up emotion. Helped cause the Spanish-American War. 'You furnish the pictures, I'll furnish the war.'
Roosevelt Corollary (1904)
👆 Tap to flip
Extended the Monroe Doctrine: U.S. claimed the RIGHT TO INTERVENE in Latin American nations to 'stabilize' them and prevent European interference. Used to justify multiple military interventions.
Panama Canal
👆 Tap to flip
U.S.-built canal (1914) connecting Atlantic and Pacific. Cut shipping time enormously. Panama gained independence from Colombia with U.S. support — raising imperialism concerns.
Zimmermann Telegram (1917)
👆 Tap to flip
Germany's secret message to Mexico: if Mexico attacks the U.S., Germany will help Mexico recover Texas, New Mexico, and Arizona. Intercepted and published — OUTRAGED Americans.
Unrestricted Submarine Warfare
👆 Tap to flip
Germany's policy of sinking ANY ship in the war zone around Britain — including American vessels carrying civilians. Killed American civilians. Turned U.S. public opinion against Germany.
Lusitania (1915)
👆 Tap to flip
British passenger ship sunk by German submarine. 1,198 killed including 128 Americans. Major turning point in U.S. public opinion, though U.S. didn't enter war for 2 more years.
14 Points
👆 Tap to flip
Wilson's post-war peace proposal: freedom of seas, self-determination, open diplomacy, disarmament, League of Nations. Idealistic. Most rejected at Versailles. Senate rejected the treaty.
Treaty of Versailles (1919)
👆 Tap to flip
Ended WWI. War Guilt Clause blamed Germany; massive reparations; territorial losses; League of Nations created. Harsh terms contributed to German crisis and rise of Hitler.
League of Nations
👆 Tap to flip
Wilson's international peacekeeping organization. U.S. Senate REFUSED to join — feared loss of sovereignty. Without U.S., the League lacked power to prevent WWII.

The 1920s, Great Depression & New Deal (1920s–1930s)

Harlem Renaissance
👆 Tap to flip
1920s cultural explosion of Black art, music, poetry, and literature. Centered in Harlem, NY. Challenged racial stereotypes. Langston Hughes, Duke Ellington, Zora Neale Hurston, Louis Armstrong.
Jazz Age / Roaring Twenties
👆 Tap to flip
The 1920s: jazz music, new social freedoms, mass consumerism, prosperity for many. Hidden tensions underneath (debt, overproduction, inequality) would collapse in 1929.
Prohibition (18th Amendment, 1919)
👆 Tap to flip
Constitutional ban on alcohol manufacture, sale, and transportation. Unintended: rise of speakeasies, bootlegging, and organized crime (Al Capone). Repealed by 21st Amendment (1933).
Nativism
👆 Tap to flip
Anti-immigrant sentiment. Led to Immigration Act of 1924: national origin QUOTAS severely restricting Southern/Eastern European and banning most Asian immigration.
Stock Market Crash (October 1929)
👆 Tap to flip
Sudden collapse of stock prices — 'Black Tuesday' (Oct. 29). People who borrowed money to buy stocks couldn't repay. Triggered bank failures and the Great Depression.
Great Depression
👆 Tap to flip
Worst economic crisis in U.S. history (1929–1939). Peak: 25% unemployment; thousands of banks closed; Hoovervilles (shantytowns); Dust Bowl; millions hungry.
Dust Bowl
👆 Tap to flip
Severe drought plus poor farming practices turned the Great Plains into dust storms in the 1930s. Hundreds of thousands of 'Okies' migrated to California seeking work.
New Deal
👆 Tap to flip
FDR's program: RELIEF (help suffering people), RECOVERY (fix the economy), REFORM (prevent future crises). Permanently expanded the role of the federal government.
Social Security Act (1935)
👆 Tap to flip
Created retirement pensions and unemployment insurance. Still exists today. First time the federal government provided a permanent safety net for all Americans.
FDIC
👆 Tap to flip
Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation — insured bank deposits. Restored public trust in banks after thousands had collapsed. Still exists today.
Causes of the Great Depression — Multiple Factors
1
Stock market speculation: People borrowed money (buying 'on margin') to invest. When prices fell, they couldn't repay loans — banks that had invested deposits in stocks failed.
2
Overproduction: Factories and farms produced MORE than people could buy. Workers couldn't afford goods they made — wages were too low relative to output.
3
Bank failures: Banks invested deposits in stocks. When stocks crashed, banks lost money, customers panicked and withdrew savings, more banks failed — a vicious cycle.
4
Smoot-Hawley Tariff (1930): Congress raised tariffs. Other countries retaliated — U.S. exports collapsed, making the Depression global.
5
Dust Bowl: Drought plus poor farming (plowing up grasslands) created massive dust storms across the Great Plains. Agricultural economy collapsed simultaneously.

World War II (1939–1945)

Fascism
👆 Tap to flip
Extreme nationalism with a powerful dictator, government control of the economy, suppression of opposition, and racial superiority ideology. Hitler (Germany), Mussolini (Italy) — the Axis Powers.
Appeasement
👆 Tap to flip
Giving in to Hitler's demands to avoid war. Munich Agreement (1938): Britain and France gave Hitler Czechoslovakia's Sudetenland. FAILED — Hitler took the rest of Czechoslovakia next month.
Pearl Harbor (December 7, 1941)
👆 Tap to flip
Japan's surprise attack on U.S. naval base in Hawaii. 2,400 killed, 188 aircraft destroyed, 8 battleships sunk/damaged. FDR: 'a date which will live in infamy.' U.S. declared war the next day.
D-Day (June 6, 1944)
👆 Tap to flip
Allied invasion of Normandy, France. Largest seaborne invasion in history — 156,000 troops on 5 beaches. Turned the tide of the European war; began liberation of Western Europe.
Holocaust
👆 Tap to flip
Nazi Germany's systematic genocide of 6 million Jews plus millions of others — Roma, disabled, LGBTQ+, political opponents, Soviet POWs. Carried out through concentration camps and mass shootings.
Island-Hopping
👆 Tap to flip
U.S. Pacific strategy: capture KEY ISLANDS to use as military bases, bypassing heavily fortified ones. Gradually advanced toward Japan. Key battles: Midway, Iwo Jima, Okinawa.
Manhattan Project
👆 Tap to flip
Top-secret U.S. program to develop atomic bombs. Led by J. Robert Oppenheimer at Los Alamos, NM. Tested July 1945. Resulted in bombs dropped on Hiroshima and Nagasaki.
Hiroshima & Nagasaki (August 1945)
👆 Tap to flip
U.S. dropped atomic bombs. Hiroshima: Aug. 6 — ~80,000 killed immediately. Nagasaki: Aug. 9 — ~40,000 killed. Japan surrendered Aug. 15. WWII ended. Debate continues.
Japanese American Internment
👆 Tap to flip
Executive Order 9066 (FDR, 1942): forced 120,000 Japanese Americans into camps. No charges; no trial. Most were U.S. citizens. Upheld in Korematsu (1944). U.S. apologized in 1988.
The Home Front — WWII Changed America
★
Rosie the Riveter: Symbol of 6 million women entering the industrial workforce. Changed expectations about women's work permanently.
★
Rationing: Civilians limited gasoline, rubber, nylon, sugar, and meat to support the war effort. War bonds funded the military.
★
War production ended the Depression: Factories converted to produce tanks, planes, ships. Unemployment essentially disappeared by 1942.
★
Tuskegee Airmen: Black fighter pilots who flew with extraordinary skill despite racial segregation. Distinguished record — escorted bombers with zero losses.
★
Navajo Code Talkers: Native Americans used the Navajo language as an unbreakable military code in the Pacific theater.
★
442nd Regimental Combat Team: Japanese American soldiers — most decorated unit in U.S. history. Their families were in internment camps while they fought.

The Cold War (1945–1991)

Cold War
👆 Tap to flip
Ideological conflict (1945–1991) between the U.S. (democracy/capitalism) and the USSR (communism). No direct military combat between superpowers — fought through proxy wars, arms race, and diplomacy.
Containment
👆 Tap to flip
U.S. policy to PREVENT the spread of communism — not roll back existing communist governments. Outlined in the Truman Doctrine (1947). Shaped every major U.S. foreign policy decision for 40+ years.
Truman Doctrine (1947)
👆 Tap to flip
U.S. would support any nation threatened by communist takeover with economic and military aid. First applied to Greece and Turkey. Beginning of containment and Cold War foreign policy.
Marshall Plan (1948)
👆 Tap to flip
U.S. provided $13 billion to rebuild Western Europe. Prevented desperate populations from turning to communism. Also opened markets for U.S. goods. Western Europe recovered rapidly.
NATO
👆 Tap to flip
North Atlantic Treaty Organization — military alliance of Western democracies: 'An attack on one is an attack on all.' Created 1949. Still exists today with 32 member nations.
Korean War (1950–1953)
👆 Tap to flip
North Korea (communist) invaded South Korea. U.S. led UN forces to defend South Korea. Ended in STALEMATE at the 38th parallel. First military test of containment. 'The Forgotten War.'
McCarthyism
👆 Tap to flip
Senator McCarthy's anti-communist 'witch hunt' (early 1950s). Accused hundreds of Americans with little evidence. Violated civil liberties. Eventually discredited after televised Army hearings.
Cuban Missile Crisis (1962)
👆 Tap to flip
USSR placed nuclear missiles in Cuba. JFK ordered a naval blockade. 13 days of crisis. Soviets agreed to remove missiles. CLOSEST the world came to nuclear war. Hotline established.
Vietnam War (1955–1975)
👆 Tap to flip
U.S. fought to contain communism in Southeast Asia. 58,000 Americans killed. Massive domestic protests. U.S. withdrew 1973. South Vietnam fell 1975 — containment FAILED here.
Détente
👆 Tap to flip
Nixon's policy of reducing Cold War tensions through diplomacy. Opened diplomatic relations with communist China (1972). Signed SALT arms treaties with USSR.
Reagan Doctrine
👆 Tap to flip
Reagan supported anti-communist rebels worldwide; massive military buildup; called USSR the 'evil empire'; 'Mr. Gorbachev, tear down this wall!' (1987). USSR collapsed 1991.
Why Did the Cold War Happen?
1
Ideological conflict: U.S. believed in democracy and free-market capitalism. USSR believed in communism. Each saw the other's system as a threat.
2
Nuclear weapons: Both had atomic bombs by 1949. Mutually Assured Destruction (MAD) kept the Cold War 'cold' — neither side could attack without guaranteeing its own destruction.
3
Post-war power vacuum: WWII left Europe devastated. Both superpowers rushed to fill the vacuum — the U.S. supporting Western democracies, USSR establishing satellite states in Eastern Europe.

The Civil Rights Movement (1950s–1970s)

Segregation
👆 Tap to flip
Forced separation of races in schools, transportation, restaurants, parks, hospitals. Enforced by Jim Crow laws in the South and de facto discrimination in the North.
NAACP
👆 Tap to flip
National Association for the Advancement of Colored People (founded 1909). Fought segregation through the COURTS. Thurgood Marshall argued Brown v. Board. He later became the first Black Supreme Court Justice.
Brown v. Board of Education (1954)
👆 Tap to flip
Unanimous ruling: racial segregation in public schools violates the 14th Amendment's Equal Protection Clause. Overturned Plessy v. Ferguson. Chief Justice Warren wrote the opinion.
Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955–56)
👆 Tap to flip
Rosa Parks arrested for refusing to give up her bus seat. Black community boycotted buses for 381 days — causing financial hardship. Result: bus segregation ruled unconstitutional. MLK emerged as national leader.
Civil Disobedience
👆 Tap to flip
Deliberately and PEACEFULLY breaking unjust laws to draw attention to injustice. MLK's primary strategy — inspired by Gandhi's independence movement in India. Getting arrested was part of the strategy.
Sit-Ins (1960)
👆 Tap to flip
Students sat at segregated lunch counters and refused to leave when denied service. Started Feb. 1, 1960 in Greensboro, NC. Spread to 54 cities in 9 states within weeks. Led to SNCC formation.
Freedom Riders (1961)
👆 Tap to flip
Integrated groups who rode interstate buses into the Deep South to test Supreme Court desegregation rulings. Faced brutal violent attacks in Alabama. Federal government eventually forced to enforce desegregation.
March on Washington (August 28, 1963)
👆 Tap to flip
250,000 people gathered at the Lincoln Memorial. MLK delivered 'I Have a Dream' speech. Pressured Congress to act on civil rights legislation. Largest political demonstration in American history to that point.
Civil Rights Act (1964)
👆 Tap to flip
Banned discrimination based on RACE, COLOR, RELIGION, SEX, or NATIONAL ORIGIN in employment and public accommodations. Made Jim Crow laws illegal nationwide. Signed by LBJ.
Voting Rights Act (1965)
👆 Tap to flip
Banned discriminatory voting practices — literacy tests, poll taxes, intimidation. Federal oversight of elections. Black voter registration in the South SOARED almost immediately after passage.
24th Amendment (1964)
👆 Tap to flip
Abolished POLL TAXES in federal elections. Poll taxes were fees required to vote — used to prevent poor Black voters from voting even when technically allowed by the 15th Amendment.
Key Civil Rights Leaders
Martin Luther King Jr.
Nonviolent Resistance Leader
Led SCLC. Birmingham Campaign. March on Washington. Nobel Peace Prize (1964). Letter from Birmingham Jail. Assassinated April 4, 1968.
Rosa Parks
Montgomery Bus Boycott
Refused to give up bus seat December 1, 1955. Arrest sparked 381-day boycott. Called 'the mother of the Civil Rights Movement.'
Thurgood Marshall
NAACP Attorney → Supreme Court
Argued Brown v. Board of Education. First Black Supreme Court Justice (appointed by LBJ, 1967).
John Lewis
SNCC / Freedom Riders
Led SNCC. 'Bloody Sunday' Selma march — beaten by police on Edmund Pettus Bridge. Later U.S. Congressman.
Malcolm X
Black Nationalism
Advocated Black pride and self-defense (Nation of Islam). Later moderated views, was assassinated 1965. Offered different strategy from MLK.
Medgar Evers
NAACP Field Secretary
Voter registration drives in Mississippi. Assassinated June 12, 1963 — the night JFK gave his civil rights speech.

Strategies of the Civil Rights Movement
Nonviolent Direct Action (MLK / SCLC)
Legal Strategy (NAACP / Marshall)
  • Sit-ins, marches, boycotts, Freedom Rides
  • Deliberately get arrested to show injustice
  • Provoke violent response — shown on TV — to gain sympathy
  • Birmingham (1963): fire hoses and dogs on children → forced federal action
  • Inspired by Gandhi's methods in India
  • File lawsuits in federal courts
  • Challenge segregation as unconstitutional under 14th Amendment
  • Build legal precedents one case at a time

  • Slower but produced lasting legal change

Contemporary America (1970s–Present)

Watergate Scandal (1972–74)
👆 Tap to flip
Nixon's staff broke into Democratic headquarters. Nixon covered it up using executive power. Tapes revealed his involvement. Nixon resigned August 9, 1974 — FIRST presidential resignation. 'No one is above the law.'
Title IX (1972)
👆 Tap to flip
Federal law prohibiting sex discrimination in education programs receiving federal funds. Dramatically expanded women's athletics, medical schools, law schools. Before Title IX: almost no women's sports in high school or college.
Equal Rights Amendment (ERA)
👆 Tap to flip
Proposed amendment guaranteeing equal rights regardless of sex. Passed Congress in 1972 but failed ratification (needed 38 states; only 35 by deadline). Still debated today.
Energy Crisis (1973)
👆 Tap to flip
OPEC oil embargo (Arab nations) in response to U.S. support for Israel. Gas shortages, long lines at stations. Revealed dangerous U.S. dependence on foreign oil.
Iranian Hostage Crisis (1979–81)
👆 Tap to flip
Iranian students seized U.S. Embassy in Tehran; held 52 Americans hostage for 444 days. Carter's failed rescue attempt damaged his presidency. Hostages released on Reagan's inauguration day.
Reaganomics (Supply-Side Economics)
👆 Tap to flip
Cut income taxes (especially for wealthy) and corporate taxes, deregulate businesses, reduce government social spending. Theory: prosperity 'trickles down' to everyone. Critics called it 'trickle-down economics.'
September 11, 2001
👆 Tap to flip
Al-Qaeda terrorists hijacked 4 planes: 2 into World Trade Center, 1 into Pentagon, 1 crashed in Pennsylvania. ~3,000 killed. Largest terrorist attack on U.S. soil. Transformed U.S. foreign and domestic policy.
USA PATRIOT Act (2001)
👆 Tap to flip
Post-9/11 law dramatically expanding government surveillance powers. Civil liberties controversy — critics said it violated 4th Amendment privacy protections.
Department of Homeland Security
👆 Tap to flip
New federal agency created after 9/11 to coordinate domestic security. Largest reorganization of the federal government since the Department of Defense was created.
Great Recession (2008)
👆 Tap to flip
Financial crisis from collapse of the mortgage and housing bubble. Banks that had invested in risky mortgage-backed securities failed. Government bailouts ($700 billion TARP). Worst economic crisis since the Great Depression.
